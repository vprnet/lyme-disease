Lyme Disease:
The Quiet Epidemic

Forty years ago Lyme Disease didn't even have a name. Today, the neurodegenerative disease infects up to 300,000 annually and has greatly expanded its range beyond the small Connecticut town where it was first identified in 1975. While the data doesn't go that far back, the numbers from the last 15 years paint an alarming picture of skyrocketing infection rates and a disease on the march.

The story of the growth of Lyme Disease is really the story of the deer tick, the primary vector for the disease and the beneficiary of a warming climate and increasingly fragmented forests. Where the deer tick and its small mammal hosts are found, so too is Lyme Disease.

Northern New England: From (Near) Zero To Endemic

New Hampshire has been hit the hardest in the last 15 years with the highest incident and growth rates of any state in the country, while Massachusetts has by far the most cases each year in the region. Rhode Island is the only state in New England to see its growth rate go down and it has by far the fewest number of cases of all the New England states.

Vermont: Facing A New Reality

Vermont's total number of cases doesn't compare to some of it's more populous neighbors, but with the third highest incident and growth rates in the nation, Vermonters now need to be tick aware and learn both how to avoid being bitten and what to do in the event of a bite.

What About The Rest Of The Country?

Lyme Disease is largely restricted to the Northeast, the Mid-Atlantic and the Upper Midwest, with 95% of cases coming from just 13 states. Pennsylvania has the most cases every year with over 5,000 probable cases in 2012. Outside of the Northeast cluster Wisconsin has the largest number of probable cases in 2012 with 1766.

While the deer tick's range extends into the South, Lyme Disease hasn't proliferated in the region the way that it has in the Northeast and Upper Midwest. This is due to different feeding habits of the ticks in that part of their range.

Since the data on reported cases is sourced by state of residence, not state of exposure, it is difficult to determine the exact origin of many of the cases. It is reasonable to assume that over-reporting occurs in the disease's non-endemic areas, such as the West.

A Note About The Data

Due to reporting methods and the limitations of surveillance data, discrepancies between figures exist, even in this report, specifically between Vermont case numbers between the New England section and the Vermont section.

In the table of New England states, the number of cases in 2012 is reported as 386 while in the map of Vermont counties, the 2012 figure is listed as 367. The figure in the table represents the number of confirmed cases in Vermont, regardless of origin. In the map of Vermont, the figure used is the number of confirmed and probable cases of Vermont origin.

Since more accurate information eliminating cases of unknown origin was available for Vermont, that data was used to provide the most accurate picture of Vermont's Lyme Disease statistics. The same data wasn't available for all New England states, so in comparing Vermont to the other New England states, the more general figures were used.

For more on the complexities and limitations of this data, see this CDC explanation.
